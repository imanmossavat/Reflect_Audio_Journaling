This week was more stressful than I expected. At the beginning of the week I felt somewhat motivated, but that motivation quickly turned into uncertainty once I started thinking more seriously about the internship project. The project is still quite open-ended, which initially sounded like a good opportunity to explore ideas. However, the lack of a clear starting point made it harder for me to decide where to begin. I realized that I tend to struggle most when the first steps of a task are not clearly defined.

A major source of stress this week was a meeting with my mentor. I had prepared several early ideas about possible approaches for the project. They were not extremely detailed, but I thought they were reasonable starting points for discussion. During the meeting, however, my mentor pushed back quite strongly on several aspects of the approach I suggested. Some of the ideas were dismissed fairly quickly, and the conversation shifted toward the possibility that the project direction might need to change significantly.

Although the feedback may not have been entirely negative, the conversation made me feel uncertain about whether I had misunderstood the expectations for the project. After the meeting, I kept thinking about what had been said and wondering whether I should have prepared differently. I questioned whether I had done enough research beforehand or whether I had moved too quickly toward proposing solutions.

Another difficulty this week was dealing with the ambiguity of the project. I spent a lot of time thinking about different possible directions but struggled to commit to one of them. I would review documentation, write notes, and sketch possible approaches, but then hesitate to move forward because I was unsure if the direction would ultimately be accepted. This created a situation where I felt mentally busy but did not feel like I was making real progress.

As the week continued, this hesitation started to create more stress. The more I thought about the project without taking concrete steps, the more pressure I felt about falling behind. Even though the internship has only recently started, I already felt like I should have something more tangible to show.

I also noticed that decision making becomes difficult for me when several possible options exist. I tend to spend a lot of time comparing different directions and trying to determine which one is the most correct before starting. While this can seem careful in theory, in practice it can slow down progress significantly.

By the end of the week, I tried to reflect on the situation more calmly. It became easier to see that the pushback from my mentor might not mean that the ideas were completely wrong. Instead, it may simply indicate that the project still needs clearer boundaries or a different starting point. Strong feedback early on could actually help avoid larger issues later.

I also realized that my approach to starting the project may need to change. Instead of trying to design a full project structure from the beginning, it may be better to focus on a small and concrete starting task. Even a simple prototype or experiment could create something tangible that can be discussed and improved.

Another improvement could be communicating more frequently with my mentor during the early stages of the work. Short check-ins or quick demonstrations of early progress might help ensure that the project stays aligned with expectations.

Overall, this week felt mentally exhausting even though the visible output was limited. Much of the energy went into thinking, analyzing, and worrying about the correct direction.
