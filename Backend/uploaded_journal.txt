This week was more stressful than I expected. At the beginning of the week I felt somewhat motivated, but that motivation quickly turned into uncertainty once I started thinking more seriously about the project I am supposed to work on during the internship. The project itself is still somewhat open-ended, and while that initially sounded like a good opportunity to explore ideas, it has actually made it harder for me to decide where to begin. I realized that I tend to struggle most when the starting point is not clearly defined.

A large part of my stress this week came from a meeting with my mentor. I had prepared some early ideas about how the project could be approached. They were not extremely detailed yet, but I thought they were reasonable starting points that could be discussed and improved. During the meeting, however, my mentor pushed back quite strongly on several aspects of the approach I suggested. The reaction felt more critical than I expected. Some of the ideas were dismissed fairly quickly, and the conversation shifted toward the idea that the project direction might need to change significantly.

The feedback itself may not have been entirely negative, but the way the conversation unfolded made it difficult for me to immediately see it as constructive. At the moment it mostly made me feel uncertain about whether I had misunderstood the expectations for the project. After the meeting ended, I kept thinking about what had been said and wondering whether I should have approached the preparation differently. I started questioning whether I had done enough research beforehand or whether I had simply tried to move too quickly toward a potential solution.

Another issue that became very clear this week is how difficult it can be to start working on a project when there is still a lot of ambiguity. I noticed that I spent a lot of time thinking about possible directions but not enough time actually committing to one of them. I would open my notes, read through documentation, sketch possible approaches, and then hesitate to move forward because I was unsure if that path would ultimately be accepted. This hesitation created a situation where I felt busy and mentally engaged with the problem, but at the same time it did not feel like real progress was being made.

As the week continued, this feeling started to turn into stress. The more I thought about the project without taking concrete steps, the more pressure I felt about falling behind. Even though the internship has only recently started, I already felt like I should have something more tangible to show. This pressure made it harder to concentrate because every task started to feel like it carried too much importance.

The interaction with my mentor also stayed in my mind throughout the week. I kept replaying parts of the conversation and analyzing what might have led to such a strong pushback. It is possible that the mentor simply wanted to challenge the assumptions behind my ideas or encourage a different way of thinking. However, in the moment it mostly felt like a signal that something about my approach was not aligned with their expectations. That realization made me feel like the project might require more adjustments than I originally thought.

One of the main difficulties I noticed in myself is related to decision making. When several possible directions exist, I tend to spend a lot of time comparing them and trying to determine which one is the most correct or efficient before starting. In theory this sounds like a careful approach, but in practice it can slow down the process significantly. Instead of experimenting and learning from small steps, I end up staying in the planning phase for too long.

This week made me realize that this habit can be problematic in environments where experimentation is expected. In a research or development setting, the first attempt is rarely perfect. Often the goal is simply to try something, observe the results, and then iterate. Waiting until everything feels completely justified might actually prevent progress rather than improve it.

Despite the stress, I tried to reflect on the situation toward the end of the week. After stepping back and thinking about the conversation with my mentor more calmly, it became easier to see that the pushback might not necessarily mean that the ideas were entirely wrong. It might simply indicate that the project still needs clearer boundaries or a different starting point. Sometimes strong feedback early on can help prevent larger issues later.

I also realized that my approach to starting the project may need to change. Instead of trying to design a complete structure for the project from the beginning, it might be better to focus on a small and concrete starting task. Even a simple prototype or experiment could help create something tangible that can be discussed and improved. That would probably lead to more productive conversations than presenting only theoretical ideas.

Another change that might help is communicating more frequently with my mentor during the early stages of the work. This week showed how easy it is for expectations to diverge when ideas are developed independently for too long. Short check-ins or quick demonstrations of early work might make it easier to keep the project aligned with what is expected.

Looking back, the week felt mentally exhausting even though the amount of visible output was not very large. A lot of the energy went into thinking, analyzing, and worrying about whether the direction I was considering was the correct one. This type of stress is different from being busy with concrete tasks because it comes from uncertainty rather than workload.

However, the experience also highlighted some useful lessons. One of them is that starting imperfectly is often better than waiting for perfect clarity. Another is that feedback, even when it feels uncomfortable, can reveal important gaps in understanding. The challenge is learning how to process that feedback without letting it completely disrupt confidence or motivation.

Going into the next week, I want to focus on taking more decisive small steps instead of trying to solve the entire project structure at once. If I can create even a small piece of progress early on, it may help reduce the feeling of being stuck at the starting line. It would also provide something concrete to discuss with my mentor, which could lead to more constructive feedback.

Overall, this week was challenging and somewhat stressful, but it also forced me to reflect on how I approach uncertain problems. The beginning of a project is rarely comfortable because it involves many unknowns. Learning how to move forward despite that uncertainty is probably one of the most important skills to develop during this internship.
